Internet Detective Academy · Space Division · Case File

The Lost Signal from Probe Aurora

Agent briefing — read carefully before you begin
⏱ Estimated case time: 50–70 minutes
Years ago, scientists launched a little space probe named Aurora to explore the edge of our solar system. One day its radio went silent, and everyone thought Aurora was lost forever. But last night — after all this time — Aurora started beeping again! Its messages are scrambled, and its batteries are almost empty. Solve the missions one by one to decode Aurora's signals and discover the amazing thing it found out there, before it fades for good.
MISSION 01
EASY

Aurora's First Photo

Aurora's camera snapped one last photo of deep space before its lens froze over. Most of the tiny white dots are just stars. But a few objects in the photo are comets — and you can always spot a comet by the long, bright tail streaming behind it.

Look carefully across the whole photo — corners too. How many comets (objects with a tail) do you count?

Stars are just dots. A comet always drags a bright tail behind it.
Hint 1

Ignore all the plain dots — those are stars. Count only the objects with a streak or tail trailing away from them. There is one near each corner of the photo, so check the edges carefully.

MISSION 02
EASY

Who Went First?

Aurora is not the first thing humans ever sent into space. That honour belongs to a shiny metal ball — about the size of a beach ball — launched in the year 1957. It beeped as it circled the Earth, and people all over the world could hear it on their radios.

Your internet task: Search the web for "first artificial satellite 1957" and find its name. Type the name (it is one short word).

Hint 1

Try searching: first satellite launched in 1957. It was launched by the Soviet Union (the USSR), and its name begins with the letter S.

💡 Did you know? That very first satellite, Sputnik 1, was only about the size of a beach ball — 58 cm across — yet in 1957 its radio beeps could be heard by people all around the world.

MISSION 03
MEDIUM

Aurora's Dutch Control Panel

Part of Aurora was built in the Netherlands, so some of its buttons are labelled in Dutch. Here are four Dutch space words. Three of them use the article "de". Only one special word uses "het" instead.

The four words are: planeet (planet), maan (moon), raket (rocket), heelal (universe / all of space).

Which one word goes with "het"? (Type the Dutch word.)

Hint 1

Say each word out loud with "de" and then with "het". Three of them sound right with "de". The odd one out is the big word that means the whole universe.

MISSION 04
MEDIUM

The Speed-of-Light Lock

To wake up its main computer, Aurora needs a secret number code. Here is the clue it gives you:

Light from the Sun takes about 8 minutes to travel all the way to Earth. Detectives round that to a neat 8 minutes.

Your code is the answer to this: how many seconds are there in exactly 8 minutes? (Type just the number.)

Hint 1

One minute is 60 seconds. So 8 minutes is 8 × 60. Work that out and you have the code.

💡 Did you know? The footprints astronauts left on the Moon could stay there for millions of years. The Moon has almost no wind and no rain, so there is almost nothing to wipe them away.

MISSION 05
HARD

The First Flyer

Aurora's log mentions a famous "cousin" robot — the first machine ever to fly like a helicopter on another planet. In 2021, NASA's Perseverance rover carried this tiny helicopter to Mars, and it lifted off into the thin Martian air. It was only about the size of a tissue box, but it made history.

Your internet task: Search for the name of NASA's little Mars helicopter. Type its one-word name.

Hint 1

Search: first helicopter to fly on Mars 2021. It flew above the Perseverance rover in a place called Jezero Crater.

Hint 2

Its name starts with "In-" and means cleverness or inventiveness — the kind of word you'd use for a really smart, clever idea.

MISSION 06
HARD

Which Planet Did She Study?

Three of Aurora's crew — Mila, Sam and Tom — each studied one planet: Mars, Venus or Jupiter. Use the clues to work out who studied Jupiter, the giant planet with the Great Red Spot.

Clue 1: Sam studied the hottest planet in the solar system.
Clue 2: Tom studied the planet that has exactly two small moons.
Clue 3: Each crew member studied a different planet.

Who studied Jupiter? (Type the crew member's first name.) Not sure which planet is the hottest, or which one has two moons? You're allowed to look it up!

Hint 1

Start with the facts you can check online. Which of these three planets is the hottest? Which one has two little moons? Once you know those two, only one crew member is left.

Hint 2

The hottest planet is Venus, so that's Sam's. The planet with two moons (they are called Phobos and Deimos) is Mars, so that's Tom's. Only one crew member and one planet have not been used yet.

💡 Did you know? Jupiter's Great Red Spot is a giant storm that has been swirling for hundreds of years — and it is so wide that the whole planet Earth could fit inside it.

MISSION 07
AHA!

The Beacon's Secret Word

Aurora's beacon keeps flashing one secret word — but every letter was pushed 3 steps forward in the alphabet to hide it. To read the real word, shift each letter 3 steps back (so D → A, E → B, F → C, and so on).

The hidden word flashing from Aurora is:

F R P H W

What real word appears when you decode it? (Type the word.)

Hint 1

Write out the alphabet: A B C D E F G … For each letter, count 3 steps backwards. For example, F → (E, D, C) → C. Now do the same for R, P, H and W.

Hint 2

Decode them one at a time: F → C, R → O. Keep going with P, then H, then W, and read the five letters together.

GRAND FINALE
FINALE

Aurora's Final Gift

You've done it, Detective. Aurora's last message is almost clear. It says it found the brand-new space object you decoded in Mission 07 — right at the very edge of the solar system — and it wants to name that find after the one thing that powered your whole investigation.

Aurora sends you nine scrambled letters:

V R E Y D I S C O

Unscramble them into one 9-letter English word that means "finding something no one has ever found before." Type that word to receive Aurora's final gift.

Hint 1

The word starts with "D". Think about what an explorer or a detective does when they find something completely new that nobody has ever seen.

Hint 2

It is the naming word (noun) that goes with the verb "to discover". It begins D-I-S-C-O… keep going to the end.

Case closed?

Aurora's batteries went quiet for the very last time — but not before handing you its greatest treasure: DISCOVERY. Every code you cracked and every fact you hunted down is exactly what real explorers do. You didn't just find Aurora's comet; you proved you have the curiosity and courage of a true Space Detective. 🚀
