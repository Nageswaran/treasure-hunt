Internet Detective Academy · Case File

The Northern Lights Expedition

Agent briefing — read carefully before you begin
⏱ Estimated case time: 70-90 minutes

High in the Arctic, a team of four scientists at Camp Nordlys ("nordlys" is Norwegian for "northern lights") studies the aurora borealis — the glowing curtains of light caused by particles from the sun crashing into our atmosphere.

Two nights ago, a huge magnetic storm lit up the sky. Then the radio went silent. No messages. No check-in call. Nothing.

You have been called in as a Junior Detective. All that's left behind is a torn field notebook, a radio log, and a set of GPS coordinates. Follow every clue, and you will find the missing team — and uncover the one quality that helped them survive.

MISSION 01
EASY

The Empty Camp

Search-and-rescue drones flew over Camp Nordlys and photographed the site glowing under the aurora. Study the scene below.

How many brown wooden crates (the ones with wood-plank lines across them) are sitting around the camp?

Count only the brown wooden crates with plank lines. Skip the tents, the sled, the two sled dogs, the yellow shovel, the red fuel drum, and the silver instrument box with the antenna.
Hint 1

Two crates are stacked directly on top of each other near the middle of camp. One more crate sits half-hidden behind the small pointed tent on the right — look carefully past its edge.

MISSION 02
EASY

The Last Ping

Before the radio died, the camp's GPS beacon sent one final location: 78.9235° N, 11.9099° E.

Type these coordinates into an online map (like Google Maps) and zoom in. A small international research settlement sits right at this spot — home to scientists from more than ten countries, though only a few dozen people live there all winter.

What is this settlement called?

Type it as two words, using a plain letter "A" instead of the special Norwegian letter, and no hyphen: for example, "Ny Alesund".
Hint 1

Type the coordinates straight into a map app's search bar, or search "78.9235, 11.9099 map".

💡 Did you know? At this settlement's latitude, the sun does not rise at all for about four months every winter — a period called the polar night.

MISSION 03
MEDIUM

The Last Radio Call

The radio log's final line isn't words — it's Morse code, tapped out fast right before the signal cut out.

-... . .- -.-. --- -.

Decode it letter by letter using a Morse code chart. What device were they naming — the one a lost team uses to broadcast its exact location?

Hint 1

Search "Morse code alphabet chart" and match each group of dots and dashes to one letter. There are six letters in total.

Hint 2

The decoded word starts with B-E-A... it's a device that sends out a signal so rescuers can find you.

MISSION 04
MEDIUM

Four Scientists, One Shed

Camp Nordlys has four scientists: Elin, Tomas, Priya, and Jonas. When the storm knocked out the power, one of them was alone in the equipment shed, watching the magnetometer (the instrument that measures the storm's magnetic strength).

1. Whoever was watching the magnetometer was wearing a red parka.
2. Elin was in the mess tent playing cards at the time.
3. Tomas was outside walking the sled dogs, wearing a blue parka.
4. Priya's parka is orange. Jonas's parka is red.

Who was watching the magnetometer when the power died?

Hint 1

Two clues tell you exactly where two people were standing already — that rules them both out right away.

Hint 2

Only two scientists are left after that. One clue names the color of the monitor's parka. Match it to the right person.

💡 Did you know? The name "aurora borealis" was coined by the astronomer Galileo Galilei in 1619, combining Aurora, the Roman goddess of dawn, with Boreas, the Greek god of the north wind.

MISSION 05
HARD

The Weather Log

The last weather log entry, scribbled just before the power failed, reads: "Outside temp: -15°C, wind rising."

The rescue pilot flying in only reads temperatures in Fahrenheit. Convert -15°C to Fahrenheit so the flight plan is correct.

Formula: F = C × 9 ÷ 5 + 32. Type only the number — no words, no degree sign, no letter F.
Hint 1

Multiply -15 by 9 first. Then divide that result by 5. Then add 32.

Hint 2

-15 × 9 = -135. Divide -135 by 5 to get -27. Now add 32 to -27.

MISSION 06
HARD

The Torn Photograph

Tucked in the field notebook is a torn photo of an old portrait, with a caption underneath:

"The Norwegian physicist who first proved the aurora is caused by charged particles from the sun, guided to the poles by Earth's magnetic field. He tested his idea using a lab model he called a 'terrella' — Latin for 'little Earth.'"

Search online to find this scientist. What is his last name?

Hint 1

Try searching "terrella experiment aurora scientist" or "father of modern aurora research."

Hint 2

His first name was Kristian. His last name is also attached to a famous type of space electric current — search "aurora electric currents named after a scientist" to confirm it.

💡 Did you know? This scientist once appeared on Norway's old 200-kroner banknote, printed right next to a drawing of his terrella experiment.

MISSION 07
BIG AHA

Red, Green, Violet

The notebook's last full page describes three colors the team saw on the storm's worst night: a rare glow high overhead, a wide band in the middle of the sky, and a faint fringe low near the horizon.

Search online to learn which aurora color glows at the highest altitude, which glows in the middle, and which glows at the lowest altitude.

Now write down just the first letter of each color, in order from highest altitude to lowest. You'll spot a three-letter shorthand you've definitely seen before — on a screen, in a paint program, anywhere colors are mixed with light.

Hint 1

Search "aurora colors by altitude" and read a science or space-agency page. It should name three colors and three rough heights.

Hint 2

Highest up is a rare red glow. The middle band is the most common color of all. Lowest down, near the ground, is a faint blue-violet fringe.

GRAND FINALE
FINALE

Found, Safe, and Sound

You track the team to an ice cave near Camp Nordlys, where they sheltered through the whole storm. All four scientists are safe. Pinned to the cave wall is one last note, in code:

ODQRDUDQZMBD

Shift every letter forward one step in the alphabet (A becomes B, B becomes C, and so on, with Z wrapping back around to A) to reveal the final word — the one quality that helped this team survive.

Hint 1

Decode one letter at a time. The first letter, O, shifts forward to become P.

Hint 2

The decoded word begins P-E-R-S-E-V...

Case closed?

Through the storm, the dark, and the cold, the team at Camp Nordlys never gave up — and neither did you. Every code, coordinate, and clue you cracked took real perseverance. Well done, Agent.
