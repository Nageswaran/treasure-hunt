Internet Detective Academy · Case File

The Chocolate Factory Inspector

Agent briefing — read carefully before you begin
⏱ Estimated case time: 60-75 minutes

De Bruin Chocolate Factory has been making chocolate in the Dutch town of Zaandam for three generations. This week they have their biggest order ever: 240 boxes of pralines for the town's 100th Chocolate Festival, due by Friday.

But this morning, in all the rush, the factory's recept-ledger — the leather notebook holding the family's exact secret recipe — went missing. Mrs. Nel de Bruin has called you in as Junior Inspector.

Follow the clues through the factory. Solve every mission, and you will not only find the ledger — you will uncover the one quality that has kept this little factory going for a hundred years.

MISSION 01
EASY

The Ledger Shelf

The office shelf is crowded with old leather notebooks: order books, delivery logs, and recipe books, all mixed together.

Only real recipe ledgers in the de Bruin family carry one special mark: a small gold cacao-pod seal stamped on the spine. Study the shelf below.

How many notebooks on the shelf show the gold cacao-pod seal?

Watch closely — a plain silver rivet and a red "RETURNED" stamp are on the shelf too, but neither of those counts as the gold cacao-pod seal.
Hint 1

Look only for round gold circles near the top of a spine, each with a small stem line through the middle like a tiny cacao pod. Skip anything silver or red.

MISSION 02
EASY

Words on the Corkboard

Pinned to the office corkboard are four old note cards. Each one has a factory word, but the little word in front of it (called a lidwoord, or article, in Dutch — "de" or "het") has faded away:

___ chocolade  ·  ___ cacao  ·  ___ fabriek  ·  ___ recept

Three of these words use "de". Only ONE of them uses "het" instead. Search online (a Dutch dictionary site works well) to check each word if you're not sure.

Which single word uses "het"?

Hint 1

Try searching "de of het" followed by each word, one at a time. Most Dutch nouns use "de" — this one is the exception.

💡 Did you know? In the Aztec empire, cacao beans were sometimes used as real money. One record from 1545 shows a turkey hen cost about 100 cacao beans!

MISSION 03
MEDIUM

The Festival Order

The festival committee ordered 240 boxes of pralines. Each box normally costs €5.

Because it's such a huge order, Nel is giving the committee a 15% discount off the total price.

What is the total price AFTER the discount, in euros? Type just the number.

Hint 1

First find the full price with no discount: 240 boxes × €5. Then work out what 15% of that total is.

Hint 2

240 × 5 = 1200. 15% of 1200 is the same as 1200 × 0.15. Subtract that amount from 1200.

MISSION 04
MEDIUM

Checking the Rules

One torn ledger page claims: "Our festival pralines are proper dark chocolate." Before trusting that page, you decide to fact-check it properly.

Search online — Wikipedia's "Types of chocolate" article is a good place to look — to find the official rule: what is the minimum percentage of cocoa solids a chocolate bar must contain to legally be called "dark chocolate"?

Type just the number, with no % sign.
Hint 1

Try searching "minimum cocoa percentage dark chocolate rule." Look for a rule used across the European Union.

💡 Did you know? Nearly half of the world's cocoa beans — about 40 to 45% — are grown in just one country: Ivory Coast, in West Africa.

MISSION 05
HARD

Two Bean Suppliers

To finish the order, the factory needs one more 25 kg bag of cacao beans. Two suppliers sent invoices:

Supplier A: €80 for the bag. Delivery is free.
Supplier B: €68 for the bag, plus a separate €15 "handling and delivery" fee.

Add up the TRUE total cost for each supplier, including every fee. Once you include everything, how many euros CHEAPER is the better deal than the other one?

Hint 1

Don't just compare the bag prices. Supplier B's invoice has a second line you need to add in before comparing.

Hint 2

Supplier A's true total is €80. Supplier B's true total is €68 + €15. Compare those two totals.

MISSION 06
HARD

The Name on the Wall

A framed newspaper clipping hangs by the office door. Part of it reads:

"...in 1828, this Dutch family patented a hydraulic press that squeezed the fat out of roasted cacao beans, leaving a dry cake that could be ground into cocoa powder — helping launch modern chocolate-making as we know it..."

Search online to find out: what surname (family name) is credited with this 1828 invention?

Type the full surname, including the word "Van".
Hint 1

Try searching "1828 cocoa press invented" or "Dutch process cocoa." This same Dutch family name is still on cocoa tins today.

Hint 2

The surname has two words. It starts with "Van" and ends with a word that means a small town or place — check Wikipedia's page on "Dutch process cocoa."

💡 Did you know? The Netherlands imports roughly a fifth of all the cocoa beans traded worldwide, and the Amsterdam–Zaandam area is considered the biggest cocoa-processing cluster on Earth — exactly why Nel de Bruin's little factory calls Zaandam home.

MISSION 07
BIG AHA

Who Was in the Storeroom?

The ledger vanished sometime between 1pm and 3pm on Tuesday, somewhere in the storeroom, mixed up with a stack of delivery papers. Three workers were on shift: Fenneke, Guus, and Ivo.

1. Fenneke restocked sugar in the storeroom from 12pm to 1pm, then spent the rest of the afternoon at the front shop counter.
2. Guus mixed chocolate in the kitchen from noon until 5pm and never once left — the kitchen's oven-timer log proves it.
3. Ivo was due to start deliveries at 3pm, but the van had a flat tire. He spent 1pm to 3pm fixing it right outside the storeroom, ducking in and out to grab tools kept there.

Only one of these three workers was actually inside the storeroom at some point during the 1pm–3pm window. Who accidentally mixed up the ledger?

Hint 1

Check each worker's alibi against the exact window: 1pm to 3pm. Cross off anyone who was somewhere else that whole time.

Hint 2

Fenneke left the storeroom right as the window began. Guus never left the kitchen at all. Only one worker was actually in and out of the storeroom during 1pm–3pm.

GRAND FINALE
FINALE

The Ledger's Last Page

Right where Ivo left it — tucked under a delivery slip in the storeroom — you find the missing ledger. Its very last page has no recipe on it at all. Instead, following an old family tradition, it has a row of numbers:

4 - 9 - 19 - 3 - 15 - 22 - 5 - 18 - 25

The de Bruin family always numbered the alphabet A = 1, B = 2, C = 3, and so on up to Z = 26. Convert each number back into its letter to reveal the final word — the one thing this whole case was really about.

Hint 1

The first number is 4. Counting A, B, C, D — the 4th letter is D. Keep going one number at a time.

Hint 2

The decoded word begins D-I-S-C...

Case closed?

Every shelf, every invoice, every alibi, every search — they all led here. Chasing down small clues until the whole picture snaps into place is exactly what makes a great inspector, and a great scientist too. Well done, Agent — the recipe, and the festival, are saved.
