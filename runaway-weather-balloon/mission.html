Internet Detective Academy · Case File

The Runaway Weather Balloon

Agent briefing — read carefully before you begin
⏱ Estimated case time: 60-75 minutes

Every night, a national weather station launches a balloon into the sky. It carries a small box of instruments called a radiosonde ("radio" + "probe"), which radios back readings about the air. Last night, a sudden storm hit. The ground team lost the signal, and the balloon never came back.

You have been called in as a Junior Detective. Use science, maps, logic, and careful searching to retrace the balloon's journey. Solve every mission and you will find exactly where it landed — and uncover the one thing every good scientist and detective needs most.

MISSION 01
EASY

The Night Crew's Table

Ground crews always prepare a few spare radiosondes before picking one to fly. Study the launch pad scene below.

How many small silver instrument boxes (each with a thin antenna) are waiting on the table and crates?

The box already clipped under the big rising balloon is heading up — don't count that one. Count only the boxes still resting on the table and crates below.
Hint 1

Two boxes are stacked directly on top of each other near the middle of the table, and one more is tucked half behind a wooden crate on the right. Look past the red toolbox, the blue tank, the yellow ladder, and the green clipboard.

MISSION 02
EASY

The Witching Hour

The balloon in this case belongs to KNMI, the Dutch national weather institute. Search online for KNMI's own explainer page about the "weerballon" (weather balloon) or radiosonde.

According to that official page, at roughly what time of day does KNMI launch its balloon — every single day?

Type the one-word English name for that time of day (not a clock number like "00:00").
Hint 1

Try searching: KNMI weerballon of radiosonde. Look for the word that means "the middle of the night."

💡 Did you know? KNMI's radiosonde usually climbs to somewhere between 17 and 25 kilometers before the thin, cold air makes it burst — far higher than any passenger airplane flies.

MISSION 03
MEDIUM

Finding Home Base

Before you can trace where the balloon drifted, you need to know exactly where it started.

Search online for the town where KNMI has its main observation site and launches its daily balloon. What is that town called?

Hint 1

It is a small town in the middle of the Netherlands, in the province of Utrecht — not Amsterdam, and not Utrecht city itself.

Hint 2

Try searching "KNMI headquarters town" or check the Wikipedia page about KNMI. The town's name is two short words.

MISSION 04
MEDIUM

Reading the Wind Log

The ground crew's log has one entry left: "Wind at launch: southwesterly, steady."

Meteorologists (weather scientists) always name a wind after the direction it blows FROM, never the direction it blows toward. If the wind was blowing FROM the southwest, which direction would a light balloon actually travel TOWARD?

Hint 1

Picture a compass rose. A "southwesterly wind" starts in the southwest corner and pushes outward.

Hint 2

Something pushed FROM the southwest corner ends up heading toward the corner directly opposite it.

💡 Did you know? Winds are always named for the direction they blow FROM. A "northerly" wind blows FROM the north, not toward it — the opposite of how we usually name rivers or roads.

MISSION 05
HARD

Straight-Line Distance

The balloon rose straight up 12 km before the wind grabbed hold of it. By the time it burst, it had also drifted 9 km sideways across the ground.

Use the right triangle below to find the straight-line ("as the crow flies") distance from the launch pad to the burst point, in kilometers.

The right angle is where the balloon changed from going straight up to drifting sideways.
Hint 1

This is a right triangle. Do you remember the Pythagorean theorem: a² + b² = c², where c is the longest side?

Hint 2

9 × 9 = 81, and 12 × 12 = 144. Add those two results together, then find the square root of the total.

MISSION 06
HARD

Three Sightings, One Truth

Three volunteer sky-watchers radioed in a balloon sighting this morning: Fenna, Milan, and Joris. Only ONE of them really saw the runaway weather balloon — the other two saw something else by mistake.

1. The real weather balloon is white, with a small silver box and a thin antenna hanging beneath it.
2. Fenna's balloon was bright red, with nothing at all hanging beneath it.
3. Milan's balloon had a large basket underneath, carrying two people — like a hot-air balloon ride, not a weather balloon.
4. Joris's balloon was white, with a small box and a thin antenna dangling beneath it.

Who really spotted the weather balloon?

Hint 1

Compare each sighting to clue 1, one at a time. Cross off anyone whose balloon doesn't match every detail.

Hint 2

Fenna's color is wrong, and Milan's balloon carries people, not instruments. Only one sighting matches clue 1 completely.

MISSION 07
BIG AHA

Fact-Check and Find the Field

Two case notes disagree about the balloon's flight. Note A: "The flight lasts about a full day — 24 hours — before it lands." Note B: "It usually lands again within just one or two hours."

Check the same official KNMI page you found in Mission 2 to see which note is actually true. Only the correct note fits a landing spot close to home base — not far away at all.

The last radio ping before the signal died gives rough coordinates: 52.18° N, 5.22° E. Search these coordinates on an online map. A small forest village sits right there, next to a private castle that is home to a real Dutch princess.

What is this village called?

The name has two words. Type both words.
Hint 1

Type the coordinates straight into Google Maps, or search for the castle "Kasteel Drakensteyn" to find the village next to it.

Hint 2

The first word of the village name is a common Dutch word meaning "low." There is also a "Hoge" (High) version of this name nearby — but that is not the one with the castle.

💡 Did you know? The small moated castle near this village, called Kasteel Drakensteyn, was built in the 1640s and has an unusual eight-sided (octagonal) shape.

GRAND FINALE
FINALE

The Last Page of the Logbook

In the muddy field, tangled in the balloon's own string, you find its little logbook. Water has smudged every page except the last line, which reads:

CHRBNUDQX

Shift every letter forward one step in the alphabet (A becomes B, B becomes C, and so on) to reveal the final word — the one thing this whole case was really about.

Hint 1

Decode one letter at a time. The first letter, C, shifts forward to become D.

Hint 2

The decoded word begins D-I-S-C...

Case closed?
