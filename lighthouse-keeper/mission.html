Internet Detective Academy · Coast Guard File

The Lighthouse Keeper's Last Message

Agent briefing — read carefully before you begin
⏱ Estimated case time: 55–75 minutes
On a lonely rock by the North Sea stands an old lighthouse. Its keeper, Mr. Bram, has kept the light burning every single night for forty years — until last week, when he suddenly vanished. He left behind only his logbook, full of puzzles, and a promise scribbled on the last page: "Whoever is patient enough to solve every clue will find my secret, and the word I lived by." As the Academy's coastal detective, follow Mr. Bram's clues one by one to uncover where he hid his secret — and the word that kept his light shining.
MISSION 01
EASY

The Harbour Roll-Call

Mr. Bram's logbook starts with a sketch of the harbour on the morning he disappeared. Each little boat flies a coloured flag at the top of its mast. Look carefully at every boat.

How many boats are flying a red flag? (Type just the number.)

Look only at the small flag at the very top of each mast — not the boats themselves.
Hint 1

There are five boats in total. Point at each flag one at a time and say its colour out loud. Then count only the ones that are red.

MISSION 02
EASY

The Oldest Beacon

Mr. Bram admired the oldest working lighthouse in the whole Netherlands, a tall tower called the Brandaris, first built way back in 1594. He wrote: "That is where my family's story began."

Your internet task: Search for the Brandaris lighthouse. On which Dutch Wadden island does it stand? (Type the name of the island.)

Hint 1

Search: Brandaris lighthouse Netherlands island. It is one of the Wadden Sea islands in the north of the country, and its name begins with "Ter…".

💡 Did you know? The Brandaris on the island of Terschelling was first built in 1594, which makes it the oldest working lighthouse in the Netherlands — it has been guiding ships for more than 400 years.

MISSION 03
MEDIUM

Follow the Keeper's Steps

Inside the lighthouse, Mr. Bram left a treasure grid on the floor and this note: "Start on the DOOR square. Walk 3 squares North, then 2 squares East. Whatever object you land on is the key to my secret."

On this map, North is up the page and East is to the right. Follow the steps and tell me: what object is drawn in the square you land on?

Count the squares as you move: 3 up, then 2 to the right, starting from the DOOR square.
Hint 1

Put your finger on the DOOR square. Moving North means going straight up the grid. Move up 3 squares (do not count the door itself as a move). Then move 2 squares to the right. What is drawn there?

Hint 2

From the door, three squares up lands you on the left-hand column. Then two squares right puts you in the middle column, near the top. There is a heavy metal object there that ships drop to stay in place.

MISSION 04
MEDIUM

The Keeper's Dutch Word-List

Mr. Bram kept a little Dutch word-list of things around his lighthouse. Here are four of them. Three use the article "de". Only one uses "het".

The words are: vuurtoren (lighthouse), zee (sea), schip (ship), haven (harbour).

Which single word goes with "het"? (Type the Dutch word.)

Hint 1

Try each word with "de" and with "het". Three sound normal with "de". The odd one out is the word for the thing that sails on the sea.

MISSION 05
HARD

The Great Dutch Sailor

Mr. Bram loved the story of a brave Dutch sea captain from the 1600s. In 1642 this captain sailed further south than any European before him and reached a large island near Australia. Long after, that island was renamed in his honour — today we call it Tasmania.

Your internet task: Search for the Dutch explorer that Tasmania is named after. Type his last name (the surname — one word).

Hint 1

Search: which explorer is Tasmania named after. He was a navigator for the Dutch East India Company.

Hint 2

Take the name of the island — Tasmania — and remove the "-ia" ending. His full name was Abel ______.

💡 Did you know? About a quarter of the Netherlands — roughly 26% of the country — actually lies below sea level. Clever dikes and pumps are what keep all that land dry.

MISSION 06
HARD

Which Ship Came First?

Three fishing boats came home to the harbour that week: de Zeemeeuw (the Seagull), de Dolfijn (the Dolphin) and de Walvis (the Whale). One arrived on Monday, one on Tuesday, and one on Wednesday. Use Mr. Bram's notes to work out which boat arrived on Monday.

Note 1: de Walvis arrived on a later day than de Dolfijn.
Note 2: de Zeemeeuw arrived on Tuesday.
Note 3: de Dolfijn did not arrive on Wednesday.

Which boat arrived on Monday? (Type just the boat's name, for example: dolfijn.)

Hint 1

Note 2 fixes one boat straight away: de Zeemeeuw is Tuesday. So Monday and Wednesday must belong to de Dolfijn and de Walvis.

Hint 2

Note 3 says de Dolfijn is not Wednesday, so de Dolfijn must be Monday — and that leaves Wednesday for de Walvis. Check it against Note 1: the Whale really did come after the Dolphin.

MISSION 07
AHA!

The Flashing Light

On his last night, Mr. Bram flashed one secret word from the top of the lighthouse using Morse code — long flashes (dashes) and short flashes (dots). Sailors have used this code for over a hundred years.

Here is what the light flashed, letter by letter:

−·−·  ·−  ···−  ·

( −·−· / ·− / ···− / · )

Your task: Look up a Morse code alphabet chart online, decode the four letters, and type the word. (It is a place where someone might hide a secret by the sea.)

Hint 1

Search: Morse code alphabet chart. Then match each group of dots and dashes to a letter. The first group, −·−· , is the letter C.

Hint 2

The four letters decode to C, then A, then V, then E. Put them together into one short word.

GRAND FINALE
FINALE

The Word He Lived By

You followed every clue to a hidden sea cave below the lighthouse. Inside is Mr. Bram's logbook, open to the last page. He is safe — he had rowed to the mainland for help in a storm and could not get back for days. His final note reads: "You solved it all. Now unscramble the letters below to find the one word that kept my light burning every night, even when it was hard."

The scrambled letters are:

E C N A R E V E S R E P

Unscramble them into one 12-letter word that means "to keep going and never give up, even when things are difficult." Type that word.

Hint 1

The word starts with the letter "P". It is what Mr. Bram showed by lighting the lamp every single night for forty years without giving up.

Hint 2

It begins P-E-R-S-E-V… It is the noun that goes with the phrase "to persevere". Sound it out: per-se-ver-ance.

Case closed?

You did it, Detective. The word Mr. Bram lived by was PERSEVERANCE — never giving up, night after night, storm after storm. You showed the very same thing by solving every clue in his logbook. The light on the lonely rock is shining again, and this time it's thanks to you. 🗼
